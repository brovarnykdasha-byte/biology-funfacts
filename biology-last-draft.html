Cell Biology

8 Facts

The molecular machinery inside every living cell — from powerhouses to self-destruction sequences.

Endosymbiosis

Mitochondria Were Once Free-Living Bacteria

About 2 billion years ago, a prokaryote was engulfed by a larger cell and instead of being digested, became a permanent resident. This endosymbiotic event gave rise to the first eukaryotic cell. Mitochondria still carry their own circular DNA, divide by binary fission, and cannot be made from scratch. You inherited yours solely from your mother.

→ Mitochondrial DNA is maternally inherited only
Bioenergetics

Your Body Recycles Its Weight in ATP Daily

ATP is biology's universal energy currency. Each mitochondrion's ATP synthase motor spins at up to 9,000 RPM. At rest, a human adult recycles roughly 40–70 kg of ATP every day — an amount close to body weight. This isn't stored: it is constantly regenerated in a relentless cycle from ADP and inorganic phosphate.

→ ~45–70 kg of ATP cycled per day, depending on activity
Membrane Biology

The Cell Membrane is 10,000× Thinner Than a Hair

Cell membranes are not rigid walls — they are dynamic phospholipid bilayers, just 7–10 nanometres thick, packed with protein machines that control everything entering and leaving the cell. That is 10,000 times thinner than a human hair. Cholesterol, often vilified in diet contexts, is actually structurally essential for membrane flexibility and integrity.

→ 7–10 nm thick — thinner than visible light wavelengths
Apoptosis

50–70 Billion Cells Die in You Today — By Design

Apoptosis is programmed cellular self-destruction — not failure, but a critical biological feature. Damaged, infected, or aged cells activate a genetic death sequence that dismantles them neatly without causing inflammation. It sculpts fingers from embryonic webbing and eliminates precancerous cells. Without it, cancer would be far more common.

→ Essential for embryonic development and tumour suppression
Cell Division

~4 Million Cells Divide in You Every Second

Your body produces roughly 4 million new cells per second through mitosis — around 330 billion per day. Each division replicates 6 billion base pairs of DNA with extraordinary fidelity, approximately one uncorrected error per billion base pairs, thanks to proofreading enzymes and layered DNA repair mechanisms that catch nearly every mistake.

→ ~330 billion new cells produced every day
Cellular Power

A Single Cell Contains ~1 Billion Protein Molecules

Inside a typical human cell, roughly one billion protein molecules are at work simultaneously. These proteins act as enzymes, structural scaffolds, transporters, and signals — each one a precisely folded chain of amino acids. The ribosome that builds them reads messenger RNA and assembles about 15 amino acids per second.

→ ~10 million ribosomes per cell actively making proteins
Stem Cells

Some Cells in Your Body Live Less Than 24 Hours

Cell lifespan varies enormously. Gut lining cells last just 2–5 days. Red blood cells survive ~120 days. Liver cells live about 18 months. Some neurons in the cerebral cortex last a lifetime — never replaced. Meanwhile, bone cells turn over every 10 years, essentially rebuilding your entire skeleton from scratch each decade.

→ Your gut lining is completely renewed every 3–5 days
Energy Cost

The Cornea Has No Blood Supply — It Breathes Air

The cornea of the eye is the only tissue in the human body with no blood vessels whatsoever. It receives oxygen directly from the air and nutrients from the aqueous humour behind it. This transparency is essential for vision — blood vessels would scatter light and make sight impossible. It's also why corneas can be transplanted relatively easily.

→ Corneal cells absorb oxygen directly from the atmosphere
3.8 BYA

Years of Unbroken Life on Earth

Every organism alive today is part of an uninterrupted chain of reproduction stretching back 3.8 billion years. Not one link was ever broken. You are, in the most literal sense, ancient beyond comprehension — a direct descendant of the very first self-replicating molecules.

Oldest microfossils · Western Australia · Life predates the oxygen atmosphere

The Human Microbiome

6 Facts

You are not alone — trillions of microorganisms call your body home, and most of them are keeping you alive.

Cell Count

You Are Roughly Half Microbial — by Cell Count

The long-cited claim that bacteria outnumber your cells 10:1 is a myth. A 2016 study by Sender et al. (Weizmann Institute) revised this dramatically: a 70 kg adult carries approximately 38 trillion bacterial cells alongside ~30 trillion human cells — a ratio of about 1.3:1. You are essentially half microbe by cell count, which is still remarkable.

→ Sender, Fuchs & Milo (2016) · Cell · ratio ~1.3:1
Weight

All Your Bacteria Together Weigh About 200 Grams

Despite outnumbering your cells, bacteria are so tiny that all of them combined weigh only about 200 grams in an average adult — roughly the weight of a large apple. Most live in the colon, where they ferment dietary fibre, synthesise vitamins including B12 and K, and train the immune system to distinguish friend from foe.

→ ~200 g total bacterial mass, mostly in the large intestine
Diversity

Your Gut Contains Over 1,000 Bacterial Species

The human gut microbiome contains upwards of 1,000 different bacterial species, collectively encoding ~3 million genes — around 150 times more genes than your own genome. No two people have identical microbiomes. Diet, geography, mode of birth, antibiotic use, and stress all shape your microbial community in profound and lasting ways.

→ ~3 million microbial genes vs. your ~20,000 human genes
Health Impact

Your Gut Microbiome Talks to Your Brain

The gut-brain axis is a real, bidirectional communication highway. Gut bacteria produce neurotransmitters including serotonin, GABA, and dopamine precursors. Remarkably, about 90% of the body's serotonin is produced in the gut, not the brain. Disruptions to the microbiome have been linked to depression, anxiety, autism spectrum disorder, and Parkinson's disease.

→ ~90% of serotonin is made in the gut, not the brain
Immune System

70% of Your Immune System Lives in Your Gut

The gut-associated lymphoid tissue (GALT) houses approximately 70–80% of the body's immune cells. Gut bacteria are essential teachers of the immune system — exposing it to harmless antigens so it learns to distinguish genuine threats from friendly microbes and food proteins. Sterile, bacteria-free mice have severely underdeveloped immune systems.

→ GALT is the largest immune organ in the human body
Virobiome

Viruses in Your Body May Match Bacterial Numbers

Your body hosts a "virobiome" — a vast community of viruses, mostly bacteriophages (viruses that infect bacteria). A 2021 study revised earlier estimates and suggested the number of viruses in the human body is roughly comparable to the number of bacteria, meaning together microbes and viruses likely outnumber your cells by at least 2:1.

→ Mostly bacteriophages — they infect bacteria, not you

Genetics & DNA

8 Facts

The instruction manual for life — 3.2 billion letters, wound into every cell you have.

Genome Size

2 Metres of DNA Packed Into Every Cell

Each of your ~30 trillion cells contains approximately 2 metres of DNA wound into compact chromosomes using proteins called histones. The total DNA in your body stretched end to end would reach roughly 60 times from Earth to the Sun and back — not 300× as often claimed, based on updated cell count estimates of ~30 trillion.

→ ~6 billion base pairs per diploid cell (haploide: 3.2B)
Protein Coding

Only 1.5% of Your DNA Codes for Proteins

Of the 3.2 billion base pairs in your genome, only about 1.5% directly encodes protein sequences. The rest was once dismissed as "junk DNA" but is now known to carry crucial regulatory sequences, non-coding RNA genes, chromatin structure signals, and evolutionary relics. The ENCODE Project found that ~80% of the genome is biochemically active in some way.

→ ENCODE Project (2012): ~80% of genome is functional
Shared DNA

You Share 98.7% of DNA With a Chimpanzee

Humans and chimpanzees share approximately 98.7% of their DNA sequence. You share ~85% with a mouse, ~60% with a fruit fly, ~60% with a banana, and ~31% with brewer's yeast. Genetically, you are less different from a chimp than a dog is from a wolf. What makes us distinctly human is mostly regulatory gene expression, not entirely different genes.

→ Human-chimp divergence: ~5–7 million years ago
Mutation Rate

You Have ~60 New Mutations Not Present in Your Parents

Every human is born with approximately 60 de novo mutations — spelling changes in DNA that arose spontaneously and were not present in either parent. Most are harmless. A handful drive genetic diseases. Over millions of generations, this slow drip of variation is the raw material that natural selection acts upon. Mutation is both risk and opportunity.

→ ~1–2 mutations per cell division copying 6 billion base pairs
Epigenetics

Your DNA Can Be Switched On and Off Without Changing Sequence

Epigenetics is the study of heritable gene expression changes that don't alter the DNA sequence. Methyl groups attach to DNA, and histone proteins compact or loosen it, switching genes on or off. Diet, stress, trauma, and environment all leave epigenetic marks. Some marks can be passed to offspring — a mechanism once thought impossible in humans.

→ Epigenetic changes can be inherited across generations
Gene Count

A Water Flea Has More Genes Than You Do

The human genome contains roughly 20,000–25,000 protein-coding genes. The tiny water flea Daphnia pulex has over 31,000. Wheat has 95,000 genes. Gene count is a surprisingly poor predictor of biological complexity — it's how genes are regulated, combined, and expressed that creates the staggering difference between a yeast cell and a human brain.

→ Complexity comes from gene regulation, not gene count
Ancient DNA

You Carry Ancient Viral DNA in Your Genome

About 8% of the human genome consists of endogenous retroviruses (ERVs) — fragments of ancient viruses that infected our ancestors millions of years ago and were incorporated into the germline. Many are now inactive, but some have been co-opted for vital biological functions. The protein syncytin, essential for forming the placenta, originally comes from a viral gene.

→ ~8% of your genome is ancient viral DNA
CRISPR

Bacteria Invented Gene Editing Long Before We Did

CRISPR-Cas9, the revolutionary gene editing technology, is not a human invention — it is a bacterial immune system. Bacteria use CRISPR to store snippets of viral DNA and cut matching sequences if the virus returns. Scientists adapted this natural mechanism into a precise tool to edit any gene in any organism. The 2020 Nobel Prize in Chemistry recognised this discovery.

→ Jennifer Doudna & Emmanuelle Charpentier, Nobel 2020

Evolutionary Biology

5 Milestones

Four billion years of ruthless, brilliant biological problem-solving.

3.8 BYA
Archean Eon

First Life — Prokaryotes in the Primordial Ocean

Simple single-celled organisms without a nucleus emerge in warm, mineral-rich seas. They establish the biological blueprint still dominating Earth today — bacteria and archaea outnumber all other life combined. Their metabolic innovations (photosynthesis, nitrogen fixation) permanently reshaped the planet's atmosphere, generating the oxygen that all animals now breathe.

2.1 BYA
Proterozoic

The Endosymbiotic Revolution — Eukaryotes Emerge

A prokaryote engulfs a proteobacterium and, instead of digesting it, forms a cooperative superorganism. The first eukaryotic cell is born — with a nucleus and mitochondria. Every animal, plant, fungus, and protist alive today descends from this single event. It remains the most consequential cellular innovation in the history of life on Earth.

540 MYA
Cambrian

The Cambrian Explosion — All Body Plans Appear at Once

In roughly 20 million years, virtually all major animal body plans appear simultaneously in the fossil record. Eyes, exoskeletons, mouthparts, appendages, and predation all emerge together. The biological arms race begins. The trigger remains debated: rising oxygen levels, shifting ocean chemistry, predation feedback loops — likely a combination of all three.

375 MYA
Devonian

Fish Walk onto Land — The Tetrapod Transition

Lobe-finned fish evolve proto-limbs and colonise terrestrial environments. Fins gradually become weight-bearing legs; gills are supplemented by primitive lungs. The bones in your wrist are direct descendants of fish fin bones — a lineage your skeleton still silently carries. This transition set the stage for all amphibians, reptiles, birds, and mammals.

300K YA
Pleistocene

Homo sapiens — The Storytelling Ape

Modern humans emerge in Africa. We share 98.7% of our DNA with chimpanzees, ~85% with zebrafish, and ~31% with yeast. What distinguishes us is not genomic uniqueness, but what we do with it: symbolic language, cumulative culture, and — uniquely — the capacity to study, understand, and write about our own biology.

5 K

Species of Mammals on Earth — Out of 8.7 Million Total

Biologists estimate there are roughly 8.7 million eukaryotic species on Earth, of which only ~5,500 are mammals. Insects account for roughly 1 million described species alone. Only about 14% of all species have been formally described by science. The vast majority of life on Earth remains unknown and unclassified.

Mora et al. (2011) · PLOS Biology · ~86% of species still undiscovered

Brain & Nervous System

8 Facts

The most complex structure in the known universe — running on 20 watts.

Neuron Count

86 Billion Neurons — and Equally Many Glial Cells

Your brain contains approximately 86 billion neurons (Azevedo et al., 2009). It also contains roughly the same number of glial cells, which were once thought to be passive scaffolding but are now known to actively modulate synaptic transmission, manage brain metabolism, and play critical roles in memory formation and disease.

→ Azevedo et al. (2009) · Journal of Comparative Neurology
Synapses

100–500 Trillion Synaptic Connections

Each neuron forms on average thousands of synaptic connections to other neurons. The estimated total number of synapses in the human brain is 100–500 trillion — more than the number of stars in the Milky Way. Every memory, habit, and learned skill is encoded as a specific pattern of synaptic strengths across this colossal network.

→ Synapse count exceeds stars in the Milky Way (~200B stars)
Speed

Nerve Signals Travel at up to 430 km/h

The fastest nerve impulses travel at ~120 m/s (430 km/h) along thick, myelinated axons — comparable to a Formula 1 car at top speed. Unmyelinated fibres transmit much more slowly, at just 0.5–2 m/s. Myelin — the fatty insulating sheath — is the key: it forces the electrical signal to jump between nodes, dramatically increasing speed.

→ Myelinated A-fibres: 70–120 m/s · C-fibres: 0.5–2 m/s
Energy Use

Your Brain Runs on ~20 Watts — Same as a Dim Bulb

Despite accounting for only 2% of body weight, the brain consumes roughly 20% of the body's total energy at rest — about 20 watts. This energy goes mostly to maintaining the ion gradients across neuron membranes that make electrical signalling possible. During intense thought, energy consumption increases only marginally above baseline.

→ ~20% of total body energy consumed by 2% of body mass
Memory

Your Brain Rewrites Memories Every Time You Recall Them

Human memory is not like a recording. Each time you recall a memory, it becomes temporarily unstable and is actively rewritten before being re-stored — a process called reconsolidation. This makes memories subtly malleable: influenced by current mood, new information, and suggestion. Even vivid "flashbulb" memories are subject to this distortion over time.

→ Memory reconsolidation discovered by Nader, Schafe & LeDoux (2000)
Gut-Brain

Your Gut Has Its Own Nervous System

The enteric nervous system (ENS), embedded in the lining of the gastrointestinal tract, contains around 500 million neurons — more than the spinal cord. It can operate entirely independently of the brain, regulating digestion, immune response, and gut motility. The ENS communicates with the brain via the vagus nerve, sending signals in both directions.

→ ~500 million neurons in the ENS — the "second brain"
Sleep

Your Brain Cleans Itself While You Sleep

During sleep, the glymphatic system — a network of channels around blood vessels — expands and flushes out metabolic waste products that accumulate during waking hours, including amyloid-beta and tau proteins linked to Alzheimer's disease. This "brain wash" is most active during deep slow-wave sleep, and significantly impaired by even one night of poor sleep.

→ Glymphatic system discovered by Maiken Nedergaard (2013)
Consciousness

Visual Processing Takes as Little as 13 Milliseconds

Your brain can detect and categorise an image in as little as 13 milliseconds — far below the threshold of conscious perception. Yet conscious awareness of a voluntary movement may arrive up to 500 milliseconds after the brain has already initiated the action. The relationship between neural activity and the subjective experience of consciousness remains one of the deepest unsolved problems in science.

→ Potter et al. (2014) · Attention, Perception & Psychophysics
86 B

Neurons in the Human Brain

Your brain contains ~86 billion neurons, each forming thousands of synaptic connections. The total number of synapses — 100–500 trillion — exceeds the stars in the Milky Way. Every memory, emotion, and thought is a unique electrical and chemical pattern across this network, running on ~20 watts.

Azevedo et al. (2009) · Journal of Comparative Neurology · 20W ≈ a dim light bulb

Body Systems

8 Facts

The human organism, precisely quantified — from heartbeats to bone counts.

🫀
~100K
Heartbeats per day
The Tireless Pump

Your heart beats ~100,000 times per day without pause. Over 80 years, that is close to 3 billion contractions. At peak exertion, cardiac output rises from ~5 to 25 litres per minute — a fivefold increase. The heart muscle (myocardium) never rests between beats, even for a fraction of a second.

🫁
70 m²
Lung surface area
A Tennis Court in Your Chest

Your lungs contain ~480 million alveoli — tiny air sacs — whose combined surface area equals roughly 70 m², the size of a tennis court. This enormous area enables the rapid gas exchange that keeps every cell oxygenated. You will take approximately 480 million breaths over your lifetime.

🦴
206
Bones in the adult skeleton
From 300 to 206

Babies are born with ~300 bones. Over childhood and adolescence, many fuse together, leaving adults with 206. More than half of your bones — 106 — are in your hands, wrists, feet, and ankles. The longest bone is the femur; the smallest is the stirrup bone in the ear, at just 3 mm.

🩸
2.5×
Earth circumferences
Your Blood Vessel Network

If all of the blood vessels in a human adult were laid end to end — arteries, veins, and capillaries — they would stretch approximately 100,000 km, enough to circle Earth about 2.5 times. Capillaries, the smallest vessels, account for the vast majority of that length. Red blood cells often travel single-file through them.

💪
600+
Muscles in the body
The Moving Machine

The human body contains over 600 skeletal muscles, controlled voluntarily. The largest is the gluteus maximus; the smallest is the stapedius inside the ear at ~1 mm. About 40% of body weight in an average adult is muscle. The hardest-working muscle is the heart — it is cardiac muscle, which never stops contracting from before birth until death.

🔴
2M
New red blood cells per second
The Red Blood Cell Factory

Your bone marrow produces approximately 2 million red blood cells every second — around 170 billion per day. Each red blood cell lives for roughly 120 days before being recycled by the spleen. They carry no nucleus (it is expelled during development), devoting maximum space to haemoglobin — the protein that carries oxygen.

👁️
576
Megapixels (approx.)
The Human Eye

The human eye is estimated to have the equivalent resolution of roughly 576 megapixels — but this is misleading. Most of that detail is concentrated in the fovea, a tiny central region of the retina. The peripheral vision is far lower resolution. Your brain actively constructs a high-resolution perception by combining rapid eye movements (saccades) with memory.

💧
1 L
Saliva produced daily
The Underrated Fluid

Your mouth produces approximately 1 litre of saliva every day — enough to fill 2 wine glasses. Saliva contains enzymes that begin digesting carbohydrates before food even reaches the stomach. It also neutralises acids, kills bacteria, and carries growth factors that help wounds in the mouth heal far faster than cuts anywhere else on the body.

Glossary

16 Terms

Key terms from all chapters, concisely defined.

Apoptosis
Programmed cell death — an orderly, genetically encoded self-destruction sequence in damaged, infected, or aged cells. Distinct from necrosis. Critical for development and cancer suppression.
ATP
Adenosine Triphosphate. The universal energy currency of cells. Releases ~30.5 kJ/mol when a phosphate bond is hydrolysed. Recycled continuously rather than stored in large quantities.
Endosymbiosis
A relationship where one organism lives inside another to mutual benefit. Explains the origin of mitochondria and chloroplasts from free-living bacteria engulfed ~2 billion years ago.
Eukaryote
An organism with cells containing a membrane-bound nucleus and organelles. Includes all animals, plants, fungi, and protists. Distinct from prokaryotes, which lack a nucleus.
Epigenetics
Heritable changes in gene expression that don't alter DNA sequence. Mechanisms include DNA methylation and histone modification, influenced by environment, diet, and stress.
Microbiome
The community of microorganisms (bacteria, viruses, fungi, archaea) living in and on the human body. The gut microbiome alone contains ~38 trillion bacterial cells and ~3 million genes.
Mitosis
Cell division producing two genetically identical daughter cells. Responsible for growth and tissue repair. ~4 million mitoses per second in a resting adult. Distinct from meiosis (gamete production).
Mitochondria
Organelles producing ATP via oxidative phosphorylation. Have their own circular DNA inherited maternally. Descended from ancient proteobacteria by endosymbiosis ~2 billion years ago.
Base Pair
A matched pair of nucleotide bases (A–T or G–C) bonded across the two DNA strands. The human haploid genome contains ~3.2 billion base pairs; diploid cells contain ~6.4 billion.
Neuron
A cell specialised to transmit electrical and chemical signals. The human brain contains ~86 billion neurons, forming ~100–500 trillion synaptic connections with each other.
Glymphatic System
A waste-clearance network in the brain that flushes metabolic toxins (including amyloid-beta) during sleep. Discovered in 2013 by Maiken Nedergaard. Impaired by sleep deprivation.
Natural Selection
Heritable traits improving reproductive success become more common in a population over generations. The primary mechanism of evolution, proposed by Charles Darwin in 1859.
CRISPR-Cas9
A precise gene-editing tool adapted from a bacterial immune system. Allows targeted cuts in DNA at any genomic location. 2020 Nobel Prize in Chemistry awarded to Doudna & Charpentier.
Endogenous Retrovirus
Ancient viral DNA permanently integrated into the host genome. About 8% of the human genome consists of such sequences. Some have been co-opted for essential biological functions.
Phospholipid Bilayer
The fundamental structure of cell membranes: two phospholipid layers arranged tail-to-tail, forming a fluid, selectively permeable barrier 7–10 nm thick.
Enteric Nervous System
A network of ~500 million neurons lining the gastrointestinal tract. Capable of operating independently of the brain. Sometimes called the "second brain." Produces ~90% of body serotonin.
